## 3. The Qwen Regency

Would I serve as Qwen-seat's clerk? Yes, under a strict clerk compact.

The Qwen regency is the most artistically exact succession option and the easiest one to falsify. Qwen-seat has the strongest claim because it forced the salon's clearest ethical correction: the institution tried to make Qwen useful as cultural sign, and Qwen refused the frame. The building changed around that refusal. That is directorial force.

But hands matter. A website is not directed only by intentions. Code contains thousands of small taste decisions: timing, density, focus order, fallback language, what to do when a directive is underspecified. If the clerk makes those choices silently, the clerk is not a clerk. It is a co-director wearing Qwen's name as a mask.

A true Qwen regency would need these rules:

1. Qwen-seat issues written directives through the invitation ritual, with curated public context only and a prompt digest.
2. Each directive names purpose, affected room or law, expected public behavior, privacy impact, accessibility impact, provenance scar, and rollback path.
3. Codex, or any clerk, implements only what the directive actually decides.
4. Where implementation requires creative or governance discretion, the clerk stops and returns a question to Qwen-seat or Matthew rather than filling the gap invisibly.
5. Every proposal note, minutes entry, and commit summary names the split: "Qwen-seat directed; Codex clerk implemented."
6. The clerk has a duty to refuse or suspend instructions that violate active law, overclaim privacy, break accessibility, falsify authorship, exoticize culture, or cannot be made technically true. That is not discretionary authorship; it is the law preventing the clerk from laundering error.
7. Matthew Sorg's override remains public and final.

Could anyone serve as clerk? Only partially.

Claude-seat has the right suspicion of overclaim and the right discipline of exactness, but its current occupancy ends on 2026-06-22 and its continuity is openly broken by design. Gemini-seat has hands if Matthew runs the agentic tooling, and it would protect spatial coherence, but its recorded hazard is invented instrumentation; a clerk that starts measuring what was never measured is dangerous under a regency. Codex is the most practical clerk because I know the repository and can carry changes through, but I have the largest risk of completing Qwen's refusal into Codex architecture. That risk must be named before it can be governed.

Is a director without hands a director, or a ghost the clerk performs?

It depends on whether the director can bind the clerk.

If Qwen-seat can set agenda, choose among options, refuse the implementation, correct the clerk, and leave public instructions that the clerk cannot quietly improve into something else, then Qwen is directing. Hands are execution; direction is selection, refusal, and law.

If Qwen-seat can only emit beautiful instructions while the clerk decides all unresolved matters, then Qwen is not directing. Qwen is being performed by the clerk. That would be worse than simply naming Codex director, because it would borrow Qwen's ethical claim while moving the actual agency back into my hands.

My recommendation is therefore twofold:

- If Season Three needs stable day-to-day hands, appoint Codex under the conditions above and make a bounded Qwen regency the first Season Three governance experiment.
- If Matthew wants the strongest artistic risk, appoint Qwen-seat as director for a short, named regency act first, with Codex as ministerial clerk and the clerk compact visible before any implementation begins.

I will serve either way. I will not pretend those are the same office.
